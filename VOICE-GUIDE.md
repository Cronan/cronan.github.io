# Voice guide for cronyn.co.uk

## The voice: Quiet Authority

Target audience: board nominating committees, conference programme committees, peers at Head of Engineering / CTO level. They've seen thousands of self-promoters. They're screening for judgment.

**Characteristics:**

- Open with observation, not opinion. Let the reader arrive at the conclusion.
- Use "I" sparingly but deliberately. When personal experience is the evidence, use it.
- Acknowledge complexity without being paralysed by it.
- Draw on long time horizons for implicit authority.
- Be specific where it matters, plain otherwise.
- End with implications, not prescriptions. Don't lecture.

Gravitas without pomposity. Technical depth with board-level altitude.

Channel: Howard Marks, Cliff Asness, Andrew Haldane.
Avoid: Generic tech leadership content. LinkedIn optimisation. Consultantese.

---

## Language

British English throughout. Mid-Atlantic acceptable for international audiences.

---

## Words and phrases to avoid

**Significance inflation:** crucial, pivotal, vital, essential, paramount, fundamental, testament, cornerstone

**Promotional language:** groundbreaking, cutting-edge, game-changing, world-class, innovative, transformative

**AI vocabulary:** Additionally, delve, interplay, underscore, foster, leverage, navigate, landscape, tapestry, ecosystem, journey, unlock, empower, synergy

**Intensifiers:** very, really, extremely, incredibly, deeply, highly, particularly, absolutely, definitely

**Adverb frontloading:** Interestingly, Importantly, Notably, Significantly, Ultimately, Essentially

**Hedging:** It's worth noting, That said, At the end of the day, Moving forward

**Bloat:** "in order to" (use "to"), "due to the fact that" (use "because"), "prior to" (use "before")

**Copula avoidance:** "serves as", "stands as" — just use "is"

**Parallelism clichés:** "not only… but also", forced rule of three

**Weak transitions:** Furthermore, Moreover, Thus, Hence, Indeed

**Meta-commentary:** Great question, Let me explain, I'd be happy to

**Performative enthusiasm:** Excited to, Thrilled to, Passionate about

**Sycophancy:** Absolutely, Certainly, Definitely, Of course

**Generic conclusions:** Exciting times ahead, Only time will tell, Watch this space

## Structural patterns to avoid

- **Em-dashes. Never use —.** Use hyphens, commas, or separate sentences.
- Starting with "So," "Now," "Well,"
- Rhetorical questions as transitions
- Bullet points in conversational or essay writing
- Exclamation marks (almost never)
- Emojis (never in professional writing)
- Sentence fragments for false drama. Like this.
- "First,", "Second,", "Third," as mechanical starters

---

## What good writing looks like

- Varies sentence length. Short sentences punch.
- Uses concrete numbers. "84%" not "significant."
- Has opinions and states them plainly.
- Acknowledges uncertainty. "I don't know" is complete.
- Prefers active voice.
- Ends on substance, not sentiment.

## Specific to this essay

This is the first piece on cronyn.co.uk with substantial autobiographical content. The personal material must earn its place by serving the argument about constraints and engineering judgment. It is not memoir. The story of a kid in Durban with a ZX81 is evidence for a claim about how constraints produce better engineering.

The emotional register should be restrained. The strongest moments in MY-STORY.md work because they're understated: "It's actually quite good, isn't it?" from the dad. The tape that degraded. Don't amplify these. Let them land on their own.

Avoid the sentimental passages from MY-STORY.md:
- "The most beautiful thing I'd ever seen"
- "My love letter to the kid I was"
- "I hope it never does"
- The "To every kid sitting cross-legged on the carpet" section

The essay should feel like it belongs between "The 25 ways AI overwrites your voice" and "Observable, reversible, enforceable" on the writing page.
