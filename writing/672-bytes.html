672 bytes

March 2026

Castling costs 40 bytes. En passant, another 30. Check detection, properly done, knowing whether the king is under attack and filtering moves to only those that resolve it, at least 80. The entire program is 672 bytes of Z80 machine code. At that budget you don't decide what to build. You decide what to cut.

This is a chess engine for the Sinclair ZX81. 1K of RAM. A Z80A processor at 3.25 MHz. The whole thing hides inside a BASIC comment: 

1 REM ... (672 bytes of machine code)
2 RAND USR 16514

The BASIC interpreter skips everything after REM. The Z80 doesn't know what REM is. RAND USR 16514 jumps into those bytes as executable code. The 64-byte chess board lives inside the REM statement itself, doing double duty as board data and machine code area. In 1K, nothing serves a single purpose. 

Component        Bytes   What it does
------------------------------------------------------
Board Data         64    The 8x8 board (1 byte/square)
Variables           7    Cursor, move coords, best move
Lookup Tables      38    Piece chars, values, directions
------------------------------------------------------
Display            85    Draw board to screen
Input              90    Read player moves from keyboard
Move Logic        100    Execute moves, pawn promotion
AI Engine         250    Generate moves, evaluate, choose
Game Loop          38    Main loop, win/lose detection
------------------------------------------------------
TOTAL             672    Every byte accounted for

The AI engine alone, scanning pieces, generating moves, evaluating captures, picking the best, consumes 250 bytes. That's 37% of the program doing one thing: deciding what to play next. Display and input together take another 26%. Everything else, the board, the variables, the lookup tables, move execution, pawn promotion, the main game loop, fits in 247 bytes.

I wrote this in Durban, South Africa, between 1982 and 1983. Twelve when I started. Fourteen when it worked. No assembler. No compiler. Nothing that would run in 1K, and nothing I could have afforded anyway. The process was: write Z80 mnemonics in an exercise book, look up each opcode in the back of Rodnay Zaks' Programming the Z80 (624 pages, third revised edition, a Christmas present from my dad alongside the ZX81 itself), write down the hex bytes, calculate jump offsets by hand, then POKE each byte into the machine from the keyboard. One wrong byte and the screen filled with garbage. Back to the K cursor. Anything not saved to tape, gone.

No school computer club. No friends with ZX81s. Durban in 1982, not exactly a hub. Just the machine, the Zaks book, and whatever copies of Sinclair User made it to South Africa by sea mail.

The exercise book had memory budgets scribbled in the margins. Small ledgers in pencil: bytes spent, bytes remaining, what gets cut if the AI engine needs another ten. I filled pages with board representations, bit patterns for pieces (bits 0-2 for piece type, bit 3 for colour), direction offset tables. I didn't know the phrase "constraint-driven design." I thought this was just what you did when there wasn't enough room.

What 672 bytes can't afford

Castling is 40 bytes of special-case move logic: check that neither the king nor the rook has moved, check that the squares between them are empty, check that the king doesn't pass through check. Forty bytes is more than the entire game loop. En passant needs a flag tracking whether the last move was a two-square pawn advance, plus the capture logic itself. Thirty bytes. The same as the combined lookup tables for piece characters, material values, and direction vectors. These weren't features I couldn't implement. They were features the budget couldn't carry.

Check detection is the cut that matters to this argument. Full implementation means generating all opponent moves to see if any attack the king, then filtering the current player's legal moves to only those that resolve the threat. At least 80 bytes. Probably more. That's 12% of the program for something that doesn't improve the quality of most moves played.

The alternative: don't detect check at all. Let the king be captured like any other piece. Move into check and the computer takes your king next turn. The computer moves into check and you take its king. The game doesn't distinguish between checkmate and a blunder.

It doesn't need to. The king is gone either way.

The system can't afford to prevent illegal states, so instead it absorbs the consequences of those states cheaply. A captured king ends the game, same as checkmate. The failure mode exists. The failure is contained. Eighty bytes freed up for things that affect every move, not just the positions where check matters.

One loop, three pieces

Bishop, Rook and Queen all move in straight lines, just different ones. Bishops go diagonally. Rooks go orthogonally. Queens go both. Separate move-generation routines for each piece would need maybe 90 bytes. One shared loop with a bitmask controlling which directions are active needs 60.

Bishop: $A5, diagonals only. Rook: $5A, orthogonal only. Queen: $FF, all eight directions. One loop. Three piece types. Thirty bytes saved, 4.5% of the total program.

The trick came to me on the bus home from school. I'd been stuck for days on how the AI engine could generate moves for three different sliding pieces within its 250-byte budget. Separate routines wouldn't fit. On the bus, staring out the window somewhere between Durban North and Glenwood, the bitmask idea clicked. Each bit in a byte maps to a direction. Set the bits you want, mask out the ones you don't. One loop handles everything. I remember the moment because I nearly shouted on the bus.

Pawn promotion is twelve bytes. Pawn reaches the far rank, becomes a Queen. No choice of piece. In grandmaster play, underpromotion to a knight occasionally matters. In a 1-ply engine with no check detection and no look-ahead, it doesn't. Twelve bytes for the thing that almost always happens. Zero for the thing that almost never does.

The AI scans all 64 squares for Black pieces, generates moves from direction tables, scores captures by the value of the piece taken, and plays the highest-scoring move. No look-ahead. A slight queenside bias because it scans a-h and keeps the first equally-scored move it finds. It will take your queen before your pawn, but it can't see your reply.

Board display came first. Weeks of work. Had the ranks upside down for an embarrassing period. Player input next. The thinking routine was the mountain: 250 bytes of move generation and evaluation, debugged one POKE at a time.

When it finally worked I saved to tape twice, on different cassettes, before running it. Board appeared. I played e2-e4. Two seconds of thought. d7-d5. The Scandinavian Defence, which lets the queen recapture if the pawn is taken. Not sophisticated. Not random either.

My dad played it that evening. He didn't really understand what he was looking at: inverse video characters on a wobbly TV, channel 36. When the computer took his bishop, he said "It's actually quite good, isn't it?" He'd taught me chess. Now a program I'd written, running on a machine he'd given me, was taking his pieces. I was fourteen. I didn't know what to do with that moment so I just watched the next move.

I should be clear about what the repository contains. The original code is lost. It lived on a C15 cassette labelled "CHESS" in biro, which went into a shoebox, through a house move, out of Durban, and was eventually found years later with the tape degraded beyond loading. The exercise book probably ended up levelling a table in my first flat in Johannesburg.

What's on GitHub is a reconstruction. An adult's work, in the spirit of a teenager's project. Some tricks are ones I genuinely used at fourteen. Others benefit from four decades of hindsight. It would be dishonest to pretend otherwise. The constraints are real, though. 1K of RAM. The Z80A instruction set. 672 bytes. Those haven't changed.

The constraint that scales

The chess engine can't detect check, so it absorbs that failure cheaply. The king gets captured. The game ends. Eighty bytes that would have gone to prevention are spent on features that affect every position. This was a budget decision, not an architectural principle. I was fourteen, working in an exercise book. I didn't have principles. I had 672 bytes.

But the logic holds at every scale I've worked at since.

I've spent the years since Durban building financial systems, the kind where mistakes are denominated in money and regulators want to know why. The budgets are different. RAM isn't the constraint; time, attention, and operational risk tolerance are. But the underlying arithmetic is the same. You can't verify everything. You can't prevent every bad state. You have to decide what the system can afford to check and what it absorbs when unchecked things go wrong.

I've written about this before as observable, reversible, enforceable: the three properties that determine whether a system can absorb mistakes cheaply. The chess engine, for what it's worth, had all three at ZX81 scale. The board state was visible. A captured king was recoverable (start a new game). The game loop enforced the rules it could afford to enforce and let the rest go.

The AI deployment conversations I'm part of now keep circling the same question the chess engine answered in 1983. Not "how capable is the model?" Capability arrived faster than anyone expected. The question is what happens when the model is wrong, and whether the system around it can absorb that cheaply enough to be worth the risk.

The instinct in most of these conversations is to reach for accuracy. Better prompting. Better fine-tuning. Better evaluation benchmarks. These matter, the way castling and en passant matter in chess: they improve the quality of play in specific positions. But they're features at a cost. Castling was 40 bytes I couldn't spend. Perfect accuracy is an engineering budget most organisations can't carry either. The teams that are actually deploying AI into production, rather than running pilots that never graduate, are the ones that accepted this and invested in containment instead. Their systems catch bad outputs the way the chess engine catches an exposed king: not by preventing the state, but by making the consequences cheap.

The exercise book had those pencilled ledgers in the margins. Bytes spent, bytes remaining, explicit trade-offs in a fourteen-year-old's handwriting. I didn't know the vocabulary for what I was doing. I do now. Every production system I've built since has had the same ledger, in different units. What can we afford to check? What do we let through and catch downstream? Where does the budget go?

672 bytes. Every one accounted for. You can read the source or play it in a browser. It won't beat a competent player. It was never going to. It plays chess in 672 bytes, and the constraints that shaped it are the same ones that matter now, applied to systems where the stakes are higher and the budget is still, always, finite.

Ivan Cronyn is Principal Engineer and Head of Solutions Technology at Man Group in London.
